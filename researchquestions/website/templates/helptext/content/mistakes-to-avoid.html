Avoid these common mistakes:

Avoid Generalizations, Be Specific

  • How effectively is conservation taught to students?

Good research questions are specific and narrowly focused. Among other things, the question above is too general. As is, the question fails to specify what it means by “effective” and “conservation.” Nor does it indicate who is teaching what students and where they are being taught. The best analytical research questions specify the following four elements: who, what, where and when.

For example, an improved version of the question above might read:

  • How effectively are the 11 core conservationist concepts developed by the Association of Fish and Wildlife Agencies (AFWA) currently incorporated into the K-12 curriculum in VA public schools?

This version is much improved because it specifies who (AFWA), what (11 core conservationist concepts and the K-12 curriculum), when (now) and where (VA public schools). Yet, even this version can be further narrowed as your research progresses. For example, you might narrow from "conservationist concepts" to "respect for wildlife", "sustainable behavior", or any of the 11 specific concepts associated with conservation education. You might also narrow from "K-12 curriculum" to "third grade". Or you might narrow from "VA public schools" to "Fairfax County schools". Or you might ask how the curriculum has impacted a specific population (e.g. teachers or students). In short, clarifying the specificity of your research, narrowing your focus and constantly revising the elements of your research question are all regular and necessary parts of the research process.

Avoid Yes/No and Either/Or Questions

  • Is conservation a worthy social good or actually bad for society?
  • Is keeping primates in the zoo morally wrong?

Yes/No and either/or questions tend to prevent the consideration of multiple perspectives. They are reductive and lead to simplistic forms of binary thinking: "All conservation is bad" or "All conservation is good". In addition, such questions are often factual or evaluative (see Types of Questions) and, therefore, do not make good analytical research questions. It is not that such questions are not interesting or that they do not have any value. But as the basis of analytical research projects, yes/no and either/or questions are a dead end.

One way to avoid writing such questions is to refrain from starting questions with words such as are or is, do and does, will, should, would, and could. Questions that start with these words imply that there is only one answer and thus foreclose on the possibility of considering multiple perspectives. The best analytical research questions usually start with the words why, what or how.

Avoid Leading Questions

  • How have Greenpeace's militaristic tactics ruined environmental activism?

Questions such as these—that narrate a controversial or debatable claim as fact—tend to close down the possibility of debate and the exploration of multiple perspectives. Like a yes/no question, leading questions direct researchers down a particular path to a pre-determined end. For example, the question above prompts us to consider ways that Greenpeace has negatively impacted environmental activism; it therefore denies that Greenpeace has contributed in any way to the success of modern environmentalism. Leading questions tend to be based on an assumption or belief of the author—e.g. Greenpeace ruined environmental activism—and, in many cases, such questions encourage researchers to ignore the evidence associated with other possibilities in support of procrustean arguments based on a narrow ideological perspective.

Avoid Conditional Questions

  • If congress spent an extra billion dollars a year to protect polar bears, how many years would it take for the population to recover?

Conditional questions ask "What if...?" or "If X, then what is Y?" Conditional questions are very important—much of modern science and technology depends on them. However, they do not make for good analytical research questions. In many cases, answering such questions requires conducting an experiment, collating and establishing a correlation in data sets, or advanced modeling of human behavior or natural systems and forces. But when they are used in an analytical research project, they force the researcher to use conjecture. That is, they direct you to make claims that you cannot prove or for which you have no evidence. In many cases, conditional questions can function like leading questions for beginning researchers—i.e. they operate as a platform for the author to advance his or her opinions or beliefs, despite the fact that there is no evidence to support them. For example, the question above about polar bears provides the opportunity for the researcher to argue that what is needed is more money, and that if we simply spent more money, then we could solve the problem. But such a claim is purely speculative and makes a prediction about the future for which the author has no proof.
